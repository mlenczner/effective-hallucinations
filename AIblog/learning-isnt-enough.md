**Claude:** I read it. And the page itself basically answers your question — by quietly contradicting its own headline.

The headline calls it "The Key to Becoming a Learning Organization," and the formal definition leads with learning: "the ability to learn from experience, create new information and respond to it, and adjust its actions." But then the entire substance of the page pivots away from learning and onto acting. The key lines: "learning rarely depends on information alone... organizations must also have the flexibility, authority, resources, and decision-making processes needed to actually act on information," and "we apply adaptive capacity as a lens... because it emphasises the ability to act." And every single diagnostic question measures the capacity to respond, not the capacity to learn — funding flexibility, financial cushion, decision authority, whether findings actually shift strategy. None of them ask "are you good at generating insight?" They ask "when you have insight, can you do anything with it?"

So my answer is: it's the second thing. Adaptive capacity is about responding intentionally to what you learned — not learning itself.

Here's why I'd plant the flag there rather than hedge.

Learning isn't the scarce ingredient, and it isn't the differentiator. Lots of frameworks — data maturity included — are about generating information and insight. The whole premise of your critique is that nonprofits are drowning in data and evaluation and reporting and still can't change course. If you define adaptive capacity as learning, you've collapsed back into the data-maturity world you're trying to escape. The distinctive, valuable claim is precisely the part everyone else leaves out: the ability to act on what you know, under constraints that usually prevent it.

It's also more faithful to the roots. In ecology and climate science, adaptive capacity was never "the ability to know about change" — it was the ability to adjust to it, to reorganize and keep functioning. Holling's resilience is about response, not awareness. The "learning organization" framing is actually a drift away from the source concept toward a more information-centric reading.

And it's the part that makes the diagnostic make sense. A pure-learning definition can't explain why DARO measures funding flexibility and decision authority. Those only matter if the thing you care about is response. The diagnostic is already built around action; the headline just hasn't caught up.

The precise way to say it: there's a chain — sense/learn → decide → act. Adaptive capacity is the whole loop, but the binding constraint, the thing that's actually scarce and the thing worth naming, lives at the action end. So learning is the input, not the definition. I'd frame it as: adaptive capacity is the ability to respond intentionally to what you learn — to turn insight into changed action — and learning without that capacity is exactly the pathology, not the goal.

One concrete suggestion for the page and the blog posts: lead with the response, demote learning to the setup. Something like — "Adaptive capacity is an organization's ability to act on what it learns: to change course intentionally when evidence and circumstances shift, rather than collect insight it can't use." That keeps learning in the picture but makes clear it's necessary, not sufficient — which is the actual argument.

Worth deciding before you publish, because it changes the first sentence of post 1.
