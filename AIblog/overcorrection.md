**Mike:** cool. So, the next thing is, ... as per the last blog post... we were focused on this idea that adaptive capacity isn't about learning, it's about learning and then doing. I think that we went a bit too far with that. The extreme perspective is that nonprofits know what to do, and they don't put it into action. I don't know how often that is the case. What I see more generally is that nonprofits put a lot of energy into assuring existing and potential funders that their interventions are having a significant impact towards their mission. And that need, plus the lack of adequate funding - often precludes them from investigating in open minded inquiry as to their impact. Following me?

**Claude:** Following you. And I think you're right — we over-rotated.

The "they know but won't act" version is too strong. It's probably rare that an org has a clear answer sitting on the shelf and just ignores it.

What you're describing is upstream of that. The energy goes into demonstrating impact to funders, not inquiring into it. Those are different activities. Demonstration starts from the answer it needs (we're working) and gathers support for it. Inquiry starts from a real question (are we?) and risks an uncomfortable answer. The funder-assurance imperative, plus thin funding, crowds out the second.

So the gap isn't really knowing vs. doing. It's between proving and learning — and the system rewards proving. That's actually a cleaner fit with your principal-agent point: the data exists to reassure the funder, which means it can't also be honest inquiry. The two purposes fight, and assurance wins.
