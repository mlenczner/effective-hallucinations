"Data maturity" is everywhere in the sector now. Frameworks, assessments, consultants, funder requirements — all organized around the idea that organizations move through stages of data sophistication toward some mature, fully realized state. The image is of ripening: you're underripe now, but with enough investment and effort, you'll get there.

The problem isn't the investment in data. The problem is the destination. Maturity implies arrival. It suggests that if you build the right dashboards, hire the right analyst, and pass the right assessment, you'll have solved the underlying problem. You won't. The underlying problem isn't a capability gap. It's a structural one — and a different frame makes that visible in a way "maturity" doesn't.

## What climate science got right

The field worth learning from here isn't tech or business intelligence. It's climate science — specifically, the way the IPCC has built a practice of rigorous work under irreducible uncertainty.

The IPCC doesn't pretend to certainty it doesn't have. It uses explicit confidence language ("likely," "very likely," "virtually certain"), publishes model ensembles that show the range of plausible futures rather than a single prediction, and treats adaptive management — adjusting strategy as new evidence arrives — as the central practice, not a fallback. The goal isn't to reach a state where you know enough to stop adapting. The goal is to build the capacity to keep adapting well, indefinitely.

That's the frame: **adaptive capacity**. Not a stage you reach, but a quality you build and maintain. The question isn't "how mature is our data?" It's "how well can we sense what's happening, learn from it, and adjust?"

## Why the frame change matters

These aren't synonyms. They point at different problems and justify different investments.

Data maturity points at internal capability: systems, skills, staff. Adaptive capacity points at feedback loops: between the organization and the people it serves, between what you're doing and whether it's working, between strategy and evidence. You can have mature data infrastructure and almost no adaptive capacity — if the data flows upward to funders and doesn't loop back into organizational learning, you've bought expensive reporting machinery, not the ability to adapt.

The maturity frame also smuggles in a false endpoint. Organizations that have "achieved" data maturity according to a framework can stop asking the harder questions. Adaptive capacity has no achieved state — it's always a live question, which is uncomfortable but accurate.

## An honest caveat

Adaptive capacity is a better frame than data maturity, but it isn't a solution. The sector's data problems aren't primarily technical — they're structural. Who the data is for, who controls what gets measured, whose questions get answered: these are determined by funding relationships that no reframing fixes on its own.

The case for adaptive capacity isn't that it escapes those constraints. It's that it's the most honest description of what good looks like within them — and that naming the right goal is a precondition for doing the work. You can't build toward something you haven't named correctly.
