If adaptive capacity is the goal, why is it so hard to build? Two concepts from outside the sector — the principal-agent problem and resource dependence theory — explain most of it.

## Origins

The term "adaptive capacity" comes from ecology and climate science. C.S. Holling developed the underlying concept in the 1970s studying how ecosystems maintain function under stress — not by resisting change, but by absorbing it and reorganizing. The IPCC later built it into the core of climate adaptation work: you can't predict exactly what will happen, so you build systems that can respond to a range of futures.

Applied to organizations, adaptive capacity means the ability to sense what's actually happening, learn from it, and adjust — continuously, not once. It requires feedback loops that run from reality back into decision-making. And this is where the nonprofit sector runs into two structural problems that neither term was designed to name, but both describe almost perfectly.

## The principal-agent problem

In economics, the principal-agent problem arises when one party (the principal) hires another (the agent) to act on their behalf, but can't directly observe whether the agent is doing what was intended. The standard solution is monitoring: metrics, reports, audits, KPIs. You can't watch the work, so you measure its outputs.

This describes the funder-nonprofit relationship almost exactly. The funder grants money for the nonprofit to deliver some social good the funder can't produce directly. The funder can't observe the day-to-day work, so it requires reporting. The nonprofit produces data to satisfy the funder's monitoring requirement.

The result, almost everywhere in the sector, is that most organizational data flows upward — to funders, boards, and grant reports — rather than back into organizational learning. This is Goodhart's Law made structural: once a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure. The data that exists is data optimized to demonstrate performance to funders, which is a different thing from data that helps an organization understand what's working.

## Resource dependence

In 1978, Jeffrey Pfeffer and Gerald Salancik published *The External Control of Organizations*, arguing that organizations are shaped — in their priorities, structures, and behaviors — by whoever controls the resources they depend on to survive.

Most nonprofits depend on a small number of funders for most of their revenue. Because revenue comes from funders rather than the people served, the organization optimizes for what funders reward. Programs get designed to be fundable. Metrics get chosen to match funder interests. Strategy follows the grant calendar. The people the organization exists to serve have almost no leverage over any of this.

The specific failure mode this produces is the nonprofit starvation cycle: funders refuse to pay for overhead → organizations underinvest in internal capacity → underinvestment gets underreported because looking lean is rewarded → funder expectations of low overhead ratchet downward → the organization is perpetually under-resourced in exactly the areas — staff development, data systems, learning infrastructure — that adaptive capacity requires.

## Why data maturity can't fix this

Both of these are structural problems. The data practices are downstream of the funding relationship, not independent of it. An organization can invest in data maturity — better systems, stronger analysts, cleaner dashboards — and still produce data primarily for funder accountability rather than organizational learning, because the structural incentive to do so remains unchanged.

Naming the structural cause doesn't fix it. But it does clarify what you're actually dealing with, which is the precondition for anything else.
