The previous two posts named the problem. This one refuses to pretend there's a clean answer — and argues that refusal is more useful than the standard listicle of solutions nobody believes.

## Why the standard solutions fail

**Diversify your revenue toward earned income.** This assumes the people you serve have money to pay. The organizations with the worst funder-dependence problem are exactly those whose constituents don't — shelters, food banks, organizations serving people in poverty. The advice doesn't reach the problem.

**Put community members on your board.** A constituent-majority board still has to take the grant or close. Governance reform changes who nominally holds authority without changing who actually holds the money.

**Funders should change their behavior.** Some do, and it helps at the margins. But asking the powerful to voluntarily surrender power at scale is not a mechanism. It's a wish.

## The exits that actually exist

There are two genuine structural exits. Neither is available to most organizations.

The first is public provision — work the state arguably should do, funded by taxes, accountable through elections. Democratic accountability is the one mechanism where communities can actually fire the decision-maker. The catch is that most of the sector exists precisely because public funding failed or was withdrawn.

The second is member-controlled forms — cooperatives, unions, mutual aid — where the funders and the people served are literally the same people. This closes the principal-agent gap by collapsing the two roles. It works. It requires constituencies that can self-fund.

## What follows from no solution

The honest position is this: the charity model is structurally unaccountable to the people it serves, most proposed fixes don't change the structure, and the organizations most in need of structural change are the least positioned to achieve it unilaterally.

This is uncomfortable. It's also generative — "this isn't fixable from inside" opens up harder questions. What is the sector actually for? If the unaccountability is structural, is it a feature of the arrangement rather than a bug?

## Mitigation is the whole game

What remains, once you've dropped the fantasy of structural escape, is mitigation — and mitigation is worth taking seriously. The gap between an organization that faces this constraint honestly and one that pretends it isn't there is real and consequential.

And adaptive capacity is best understood in this light. It's not a solution to the principal-agent problem or resource dependence. It's the best available proxy for community accountability when the structural version is blocked.

There is no solution. Honest acknowledgement of the constraint, and careful work within it, is the whole game.
